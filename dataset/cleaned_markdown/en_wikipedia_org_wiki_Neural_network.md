From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Structure in biology and artificial intelligence

For other uses, see [Neural network (disambiguation)](/wiki/Neural_network_\(disambiguation\) "Neural network \(disambiguation\)").

A **neural network** is a group of interconnected units called [neurons](/wiki/Neurons "Neurons") that send signals to one another. Neurons can be either [biological cells](/wiki/Cell_\(biology\) "Cell \(biology\)") or [mathematical models](/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model"). While individual neurons are simple, many of them together in a network can perform complex tasks. There are two main types of neural networks. 

  * In [neuroscience](/wiki/Neuroscience "Neuroscience"), a _[biological neural network](/wiki/Biological_neural_network "Biological neural network")_ is a physical structure found in [brains](/wiki/Brain "Brain") and complex [nervous systems](/wiki/Nervous_system "Nervous system") – a population of nerve cells connected by [synapses](/wiki/Synapse "Synapse").
  * In [machine learning](/wiki/Machine_learning "Machine learning"), an _[artificial neural network](/wiki/Neural_network_\(machine_learning\) "Neural network \(machine learning\)")_ is a mathematical model used to approximate [nonlinear functions](/wiki/Nonlinear_function "Nonlinear function"). Artificial neural networks are used to solve [artificial intelligence](/wiki/Artificial_intelligence "Artificial intelligence") problems.

## In biology

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Neural_network&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: In biology")]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Projections_of_Gpr101_TomatoMSNsinSTR.gif/250px-Projections_of_Gpr101_TomatoMSNsinSTR.gif)](/wiki/File:Projections_of_Gpr101_TomatoMSNsinSTR.gif)Animated [confocal micrograph](/wiki/Confocal_microscopy "Confocal microscopy") of part of a biological neural network in a mouse's [striatum](/wiki/Striatum "Striatum")

Main article: [Neural network (biology)](/wiki/Neural_network_\(biology\) "Neural network \(biology\)")

In the context of biology, a neural network is a population of biological [neurons](/wiki/Neuron "Neuron") chemically connected to each other by [synapses](/wiki/Synapse "Synapse"). A given neuron can be connected to hundreds of thousands of synapses.[[1]](#cite_note-shao-1) Each neuron sends and receives [electrochemical](/wiki/Electrochemistry "Electrochemistry") signals called [action potentials](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential") to its connected neighbors. A neuron can serve an [excitatory](/wiki/Excitatory_postsynaptic_potential "Excitatory postsynaptic potential") role, amplifying and propagating signals it receives, or an [inhibitory](/wiki/Inhibitory_postsynaptic_potential "Inhibitory postsynaptic potential") role, suppressing signals instead.[[1]](#cite_note-shao-1)

Populations of interconnected neurons that are smaller than neural networks are called [neural circuits](/wiki/Neural_circuits "Neural circuits"). Very large interconnected networks are called [large scale brain networks](/wiki/Large_scale_brain_network "Large scale brain network"), and many of these together form [brains](/wiki/Brain "Brain") and [nervous systems](/wiki/Nervous_system "Nervous system"). 

Signals generated by neural networks in the brain eventually travel through the nervous system and across [neuromuscular junctions](/wiki/Neuromuscular_junctions "Neuromuscular junctions") to [muscle cells](/wiki/Muscle_cell "Muscle cell"), where they cause contraction and thereby motion.[[2]](#cite_note-Levitan_2015-2)

## In machine learning

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Neural_network&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: In machine learning")]

Main article: [Neural network (machine learning)](/wiki/Neural_network_\(machine_learning\) "Neural network \(machine learning\)")

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Neural_network_example.svg/250px-Neural_network_example.svg.png)](/wiki/File:Neural_network_example.svg)Schematic of a simple [feedforward](/wiki/Feedforward_neural_network "Feedforward neural network") artificial neural network

In machine learning, a neural network is an artificial mathematical model used to approximate nonlinear functions. While early artificial neural networks were physical machines,[[3]](#cite_note-rosenblatt-3) today they are almost always implemented in [software](/wiki/Neural_network_software "Neural network software"). 

[Neurons](/wiki/Artificial_neuron "Artificial neuron") in an artificial neural network are usually arranged into layers, with information passing from the first layer (the input layer) through one or more intermediate layers ([the hidden layers](/wiki/Hidden_layer "Hidden layer")) to the final layer (the output layer).[[4]](#cite_note-4) The "signal" input to each neuron is a number, specifically a [linear combination](/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of the outputs of the connected neurons in the previous layer. The signal each neuron outputs is calculated from this number, according to its [activation function](/wiki/Activation_function "Activation function"). The behavior of the network depends on the strengths (or _weights_) of the connections between neurons. A network is trained by modifying these weights through [empirical risk minimization](/wiki/Empirical_risk_minimization "Empirical risk minimization") or [backpropagation](/wiki/Backpropagation "Backpropagation") in order to fit some preexisting dataset.[[5]](#cite_note-5)

The term _[deep neural network](/wiki/Deep_neural_network "Deep neural network")_ refers to neural networks that have more than three layers, typically including at least two hidden layers in addition to the input and output layers. 

Neural networks are used to solve problems in [artificial intelligence](/wiki/Artificial_intelligence "Artificial intelligence"), and have thereby found applications in many disciplines, including [predictive modeling](/wiki/Predictive_modeling "Predictive modeling"), [adaptive control](/wiki/Adaptive_control "Adaptive control"), [facial recognition](/wiki/Facial_recognition_system "Facial recognition system"), [handwriting recognition](/wiki/Handwriting_recognition "Handwriting recognition"), [general game playing](/wiki/General_game_playing "General game playing"), and [generative AI](/wiki/Generative_AI "Generative AI"). 

## History

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Neural_network&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: History")]

See also: [Biological neural network § History](/wiki/Biological_neural_network#History "Biological neural network"), and [History of artificial neural networks](/wiki/History_of_artificial_neural_networks "History of artificial neural networks")

The theoretical base for contemporary neural networks was independently proposed by [Alexander Bain](/wiki/Alexander_Bain_\(philosopher\) "Alexander Bain \(philosopher\)") in 1873[[6]](#cite_note-Bain_1873-6) and [William James](/wiki/William_James "William James") in 1890.[[7]](#cite_note-James_1890-7) Both posited that human thought emerged from interactions among large numbers of neurons inside the brain. In 1949, [Donald Hebb](/wiki/Donald_Hebb "Donald Hebb") described [Hebbian learning](/wiki/Hebbian_learning "Hebbian learning"), the idea that neural networks can change and learn over time by strengthening a synapse every time a signal travels along it.[[8]](#cite_note-Hebb_1949-8) In 1956, [Svaetichin](/wiki/Gunnar_Svaetichin "Gunnar Svaetichin") discovered the functioning of second order retinal cells (Horizontal Cells), which were fundamental for the understanding of neural networks. 

Artificial neural networks were originally used to model biological neural networks starting in the 1930s under the approach of [connectionism](/wiki/Connectionism "Connectionism"). However, starting with the invention of the [perceptron](/wiki/Perceptron "Perceptron"), a simple artificial neural network, by [Warren McCulloch](/wiki/Warren_McCulloch "Warren McCulloch") and [Walter Pitts](/wiki/Walter_Pitts "Walter Pitts") in 1943,[[9]](#cite_note-9) followed by the implementation of one in hardware by [Frank Rosenblatt](/wiki/Frank_Rosenblatt "Frank Rosenblatt") in 1957,[[3]](#cite_note-rosenblatt-3) artificial neural networks became increasingly used for machine learning applications instead, and increasingly different from their biological counterparts.
