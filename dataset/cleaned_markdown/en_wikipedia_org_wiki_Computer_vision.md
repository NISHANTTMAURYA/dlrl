**Computer vision** tasks include methods for [acquiring](/wiki/Image_sensor "Image sensor"), [processing](/wiki/Image_processing "Image processing"), [analyzing](/wiki/Image_analysis "Image analysis"), and understanding [digital images](/wiki/Digital_image "Digital image"), and extraction of [high-dimensional](/wiki/High-dimensional "High-dimensional") data from the real world in order to produce numerical or symbolic information, e.g. in the form of decisions.[[1]](#cite_note-Klette-2014-1)[[2]](#cite_note-Shapiro-Stockman-2001-2)[[3]](#cite_note-Morris-2004-3)[[4]](#cite_note-Jahne-Haussecker-2000-4) "Understanding" in this context signifies the transformation of visual images (the input to the [retina](/wiki/Retina "Retina")) into descriptions of the world that make sense to thought processes and can elicit appropriate action. This image understanding can be seen as the disentangling of symbolic information from image data using models constructed with the aid of geometry, physics, statistics, and learning theory. 

The [scientific discipline](/wiki/Scientific_discipline "Scientific discipline") of computer vision is concerned with the theory behind artificial systems that extract information from images. Image data can take many forms, such as video sequences, views from multiple cameras, multi-dimensional data from a [3D scanner](/wiki/3D_scanning "3D scanning"), 3D point clouds from [LiDaR](/wiki/Lidar "Lidar") sensors, or medical scanning devices. The technological discipline of computer vision seeks to apply its theories and models to the construction of computer vision systems. 

Subdisciplines of computer vision include [scene reconstruction](/wiki/3D_reconstruction "3D reconstruction"), [object detection](/wiki/Object_detection "Object detection"), [event detection](/wiki/Event_detection "Event detection"), [activity recognition](/wiki/Activity_recognition "Activity recognition"), [video tracking](/wiki/Video_tracking "Video tracking"), [object recognition](/wiki/Object_recognition "Object recognition"), [3D pose estimation](/wiki/3D_pose_estimation "3D pose estimation"), learning, indexing, [motion estimation](/wiki/Motion_estimation "Motion estimation"), [visual servoing](/wiki/Visual_servoing "Visual servoing"), [3D scene modeling](/wiki/3D_modeling "3D modeling"), and [image restoration](/wiki/Digital_photograph_restoration "Digital photograph restoration"). 

## Definition

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition")]

Computer vision is an [interdisciplinary field](/wiki/Interdisciplinarity "Interdisciplinarity") that deals with how computers can be made to gain high-level understanding from [digital images](/wiki/Digital_image "Digital image") or [videos](/wiki/Video "Video"). From the perspective of [engineering](/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), it seeks to automate tasks that the [human visual system](/wiki/Human_visual_system "Human visual system") can do.[[5]](#cite_note-Ballard-Brown-1982-5)[[6]](#cite_note-Huang-1996-6)[[7]](#cite_note-Sonka-Hlavac-Boyle-2008-7) "Computer vision is concerned with the automatic extraction, analysis, and understanding of useful information from a single image or a sequence of images. It involves the development of a theoretical and algorithmic basis to achieve automatic visual understanding."[[8]](#cite_note-bmva-8) As a [scientific discipline](/wiki/Scientific_discipline "Scientific discipline"), computer vision is concerned with the theory behind artificial systems that extract information from images. The image data can take many forms, such as video sequences, views from multiple cameras, or multi-dimensional data from a [medical scanner](/wiki/Medical_scanner "Medical scanner").[[9]](#cite_note-9) As a technological discipline, computer vision seeks to apply its theories and models for the construction of computer vision systems. [Machine vision](/wiki/Machine_vision "Machine vision") refers to a systems engineering discipline, especially in the context of factory automation. In more recent times, the terms computer vision and machine vision have converged to a greater degree.[[10]](#cite_note-davies5-10): 13

## History

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History")]

In the late 1960s, computer vision began at universities that were pioneering [artificial intelligence](/wiki/Artificial_intelligence "Artificial intelligence"). It was meant to mimic the [human visual system](/wiki/Human_visual_system "Human visual system") as a stepping stone to endowing robots with intelligent behavior.[[11]](#cite_note-Szeliski2010-11) In 1966, it was believed that this could be achieved through an undergraduate summer project,[[12]](#cite_note-12) by attaching a camera to a computer and having it "describe what it saw".[[13]](#cite_note-Seymour1966-13)[[14]](#cite_note-Boden2006-14)

What distinguished computer vision from the prevalent field of [digital image processing](/wiki/Digital_image_processing "Digital image processing") at that time was a desire to extract [three-dimensional](/wiki/Three-dimensional "Three-dimensional") structure from images with the goal of achieving full scene understanding. Studies in the 1970s formed the early foundations for many of the computer vision [algorithms](/wiki/Algorithm "Algorithm") that exist today, including [extraction of edges](/wiki/Edge_detection "Edge detection") from images, labeling of lines, non-polyhedral and [polyhedral modeling](/wiki/Polyhedron_model "Polyhedron model"), representation of objects as interconnections of smaller structures, [optical flow](/wiki/Optical_flow "Optical flow"), and [motion estimation](/wiki/Motion_estimation "Motion estimation").[[11]](#cite_note-Szeliski2010-11)

The next decade saw studies based on more rigorous mathematical analysis and quantitative aspects of computer vision. These include the concept of [scale-space](/wiki/Scale_space "Scale space"), the inference of shape from various cues such as [shading](/wiki/Shading "Shading"), texture and focus, and [contour models known as snakes](/wiki/Active_contour_model "Active contour model"). Researchers also realized that many of these mathematical concepts could be treated within the same optimization framework as [regularization](/wiki/Regularization_\(mathematics\) "Regularization \(mathematics\)") and [Markov random fields](/wiki/Markov_random_field "Markov random field").[[15]](#cite_note-Kanade20122-15) By the 1990s, some of the previous research topics became more active than others. Research in [projective](/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry") [3-D reconstructions](/wiki/3D_reconstruction "3D reconstruction") led to better understanding of [camera calibration](/wiki/Camera_resectioning "Camera resectioning"). With the advent of optimization methods for camera calibration, it was realized that a lot of the ideas were already explored in [bundle adjustment](/wiki/Bundle_adjustment "Bundle adjustment") theory from the field of [photogrammetry](/wiki/Photogrammetry "Photogrammetry"). This led to methods for sparse [3-D reconstructions of scenes from multiple images](/wiki/3D_reconstruction_from_multiple_images "3D reconstruction from multiple images"). Progress was made on the dense stereo [correspondence problem](/wiki/Correspondence_problem "Correspondence problem") and further multi-view stereo techniques. At the same time, [variations of graph cut](/wiki/Graph_cuts_in_computer_vision "Graph cuts in computer vision") were used to solve [image segmentation](/wiki/Image_segmentation "Image segmentation"). This decade also marked the first time statistical learning techniques were used in practice to recognize faces in images (see [Eigenface](/wiki/Eigenface "Eigenface")). Toward the end of the 1990s, a significant change came about with the increased interaction between the fields of [computer graphics](/wiki/Computer_graphics_\(computer_science\) "Computer graphics \(computer science\)") and computer vision. This included [image-based rendering](/wiki/Image-based_rendering "Image-based rendering"), [image morphing](/wiki/Morphing "Morphing"), view interpolation, [panoramic image stitching](/wiki/Image_stitching "Image stitching") and early [light-field rendering](/wiki/Light_field "Light field").[[11]](#cite_note-Szeliski2010-11)

Recent work has seen the resurgence of [feature](/wiki/Feature_\(computer_vision\) "Feature \(computer vision\)")-based methods used in conjunction with machine learning techniques and complex optimization frameworks.[[16]](#cite_note-Sebe2005-16)[[17]](#cite_note-Freeman2008-17) The advancement of [Deep Learning](/wiki/Deep_learning "Deep learning") techniques has brought further life to the field of computer vision. The accuracy of deep learning algorithms on several benchmark computer vision data sets for tasks ranging from classification,[[18]](#cite_note-NatureBengio-18) segmentation and optical flow has surpassed prior methods.[[19]](#cite_note-19)[[20]](#cite_note-20)

### Solid-state physics

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Solid-state physics")]

[Solid-state physics](/wiki/Solid-state_physics "Solid-state physics") is another field that is closely related to computer vision. Most computer vision systems rely on [image sensors](/wiki/Image_sensors "Image sensors"), which detect [electromagnetic radiation](/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation"), which is typically in the form of either [visible](/wiki/Visible_light "Visible light"), [infrared](/wiki/Infrared_light "Infrared light") or [ultraviolet light](/wiki/Ultraviolet_light "Ultraviolet light"). The sensors are designed using [quantum physics](/wiki/Quantum_physics "Quantum physics"). The process by which light interacts with surfaces is explained using physics. Physics explains the behavior of [optics](/wiki/Optics "Optics") which are a core part of most imaging systems. Sophisticated [image sensors](/wiki/Image_sensors "Image sensors") even require [quantum mechanics](/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") to provide a complete understanding of the image formation process.[[11]](#cite_note-Szeliski2010-11) Also, various measurement problems in physics can be addressed using computer vision, for example, motion in fluids. 

### Neurobiology

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Neurobiology")]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Simplified_neural_network_training_example.svg/250px-Simplified_neural_network_training_example.svg.png)](/wiki/File:Simplified_neural_network_training_example.svg)

Simplified example of training a neural network in object detection: The network is trained by multiple images that are known to depict [starfish](/wiki/Starfish "Starfish") and [sea urchins](/wiki/Sea_urchin "Sea urchin"), which are correlated with "nodes" that represent visual [features](/wiki/Feature_\(computer_vision\) "Feature \(computer vision\)"). The starfish match with a ringed texture and a star outline, whereas most sea urchins match with a striped texture and oval shape. However, the instance of a ring-textured sea urchin creates a weakly weighted association between them.

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Simplified_neural_network_example.svg/250px-Simplified_neural_network_example.svg.png)](/wiki/File:Simplified_neural_network_example.svg)

Subsequent run of the network on an input image (left):[[21]](#cite_note-21) The network correctly detects the starfish. However, the weakly weighted association between ringed texture and sea urchin also confers a weak signal to the latter from one of two intermediate nodes. In addition, a shell that was not included in the training gives a weak signal for the oval shape, also resulting in a weak signal for the sea urchin output. These weak signals may result in a [false positive](/wiki/False_positive "False positive") result for sea urchin.  
In reality, textures and outlines would not be represented by single nodes, but rather by associated weight patterns of multiple nodes.

[Neurobiology](/wiki/Neurobiology "Neurobiology") has greatly influenced the development of computer vision algorithms. Over the last century, there has been an extensive study of eyes, neurons, and brain structures devoted to the processing of visual stimuli in both humans and various animals. This has led to a coarse yet convoluted description of how natural vision systems operate in order to solve certain vision-related tasks. These results have led to a sub-field within computer vision where artificial systems are designed to mimic the processing and behavior of biological systems at different levels of complexity. Also, some of the learning-based methods developed within computer vision (_e.g._ [neural net](/wiki/Artificial_neural_network "Artificial neural network") and [deep learning](/wiki/Deep_learning "Deep learning") based image and feature analysis and classification) have their background in neurobiology. The [Neocognitron](/wiki/Neocognitron "Neocognitron"), a neural network developed in the 1970s by [Kunihiko Fukushima](/wiki/Kunihiko_Fukushima "Kunihiko Fukushima"), is an early example of computer vision taking direct inspiration from neurobiology, specifically the [primary visual cortex](/wiki/Visual_cortex#Primary_visual_cortex_\(V1\) "Visual cortex"). 

Some strands of computer vision research are closely related to the study of [biological vision](/wiki/Biological_vision "Biological vision")—indeed, just as many strands of [AI](/wiki/Artificial_intelligence "Artificial intelligence") research are closely tied with research into human intelligence and the use of stored knowledge to interpret, integrate, and utilize visual information. The field of biological vision studies and models the physiological processes behind visual perception in humans and other animals. Computer vision, on the other hand, develops and describes the algorithms implemented in software and hardware behind artificial vision systems. An interdisciplinary exchange between biological and computer vision has proven fruitful for both fields.[[22]](#cite_note-TextbookP1-22)

### Signal processing

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Signal processing")]

Yet another field related to computer vision is [signal processing](/wiki/Signal_processing "Signal processing"). Many methods for processing one-variable signals, typically temporal signals, can be extended in a natural way to the processing of two-variable signals or multi-variable signals in computer vision. However, because of the specific nature of images, there are many methods developed within computer vision that have no counterpart in the processing of one-variable signals. Together with the multi-dimensionality of the signal, this defines a subfield in signal processing as a part of computer vision.

### Distinctions

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Distinctions")]

The fields most closely related to computer vision are [image processing](/wiki/Image_processing "Image processing"), [image analysis](/wiki/Image_analysis "Image analysis") and [machine vision](/wiki/Machine_vision "Machine vision"). There is a significant overlap in the range of techniques and applications that these cover. This implies that the basic techniques that are used and developed in these fields are similar, something which can be interpreted as there is only one field with different names. On the other hand, it appears to be necessary for research groups, scientific journals, conferences, and companies to present or market themselves as belonging specifically to one of these fields and, hence, various characterizations which distinguish each of the fields from the others have been presented. In image processing, the input and output are both images, whereas in computer vision, the input is an image or video, and the output could be an enhanced image, an analysis of the image's content, or even a system's behavior based on that analysis. 

[Computer graphics](/wiki/Computer_graphics "Computer graphics") produces image data from 3D models, and computer vision often produces 3D models from image data.[[25]](#cite_note-3DVAE-25) There is also a trend towards a combination of the two disciplines, _e.g._ , as explored in [augmented reality](/wiki/Augmented_reality "Augmented reality"). 

The following characterizations appear relevant but should not be taken as universally accepted: 

  * [Image processing](/wiki/Image_processing "Image processing") and [image analysis](/wiki/Image_analysis "Image analysis") tend to focus on 2D images, how to transform one image to another, _e.g._ , by pixel-wise operations such as contrast enhancement, local operations such as edge extraction or noise removal, or geometrical transformations such as rotating the image. This characterization implies that image processing/analysis neither requires assumptions nor produces interpretations about the image content.
  * Computer vision includes 3D analysis from 2D images. This analyzes the 3D scene projected onto one or several images, _e.g._ , how to reconstruct structure or other information about the 3D scene from one or several images. Computer vision often relies on more or less complex assumptions about the scene depicted in an image.
  * [Machine vision](/wiki/Machine_vision "Machine vision") is the process of applying a range of technologies and methods to provide imaging-based automatic inspection, process control, and robot guidance[[26]](#cite_note-NASAarticle-26) in industrial applications.[[22]](#cite_note-TextbookP1-22) Machine vision tends to focus on applications, mainly in manufacturing, _e.g._ , vision-based robots and systems for vision-based inspection, measurement, or picking (such as [bin picking](/wiki/Bin_picking "Bin picking")[[27]](#cite_note-27)). This implies that image sensor technologies and control theory often are integrated with the processing of image data to control a robot and that real-time processing is emphasized by means of efficient implementations in hardware and software. It also implies that external conditions such as lighting can be and are often more controlled in machine vision than they are in general computer vision, which can enable the use of different algorithms.
  * There is also a field called [imaging](/wiki/Imaging_science "Imaging science") which primarily focuses on the process of producing images, but sometimes also deals with the processing and analysis of images. For example, [medical imaging](/wiki/Medical_imaging "Medical imaging") includes substantial work on the analysis of image data in medical applications. Progress in [convolutional neural networks](/wiki/Convolutional_neural_network "Convolutional neural network") (CNNs) has improved the accurate detection of disease in medical images, particularly in cardiology, pathology, dermatology, and radiology.[[28]](#cite_note-28)
  * Finally, [pattern recognition](/wiki/Pattern_recognition "Pattern recognition") is a field that uses various methods to extract information from signals in general, mainly based on statistical approaches and [artificial neural networks](/wiki/Artificial_neural_networks "Artificial neural networks").[[29]](#cite_note-29) A significant part of this field is devoted to applying these methods to image data.

[Photogrammetry](/wiki/Photogrammetry "Photogrammetry") also overlaps with computer vision, e.g., [stereophotogrammetry](/wiki/Stereophotogrammetry "Stereophotogrammetry") vs. [computer stereo vision](/wiki/Computer_stereo_vision "Computer stereo vision"). 

## Applications

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Applications")]

Applications range from tasks such as industrial [machine vision](/wiki/Machine_vision "Machine vision") systems which, say, inspect bottles speeding by on a production line, to research into artificial intelligence and computers or robots that can comprehend the world around them. The computer vision and machine vision fields have significant overlap. Computer vision covers the core technology of automated image analysis which is used in many fields. Machine vision usually refers to a process of combining automated image analysis with other methods and technologies to provide automated inspection and robot guidance in industrial applications. In many computer-vision applications, computers are pre-programmed to solve a particular task, but methods based on learning are now becoming increasingly common. Examples of applications of computer vision include systems for: 

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Synthesizing_3D_Shapes_via_Modeling_Multi-View_Depth_Maps_and_Silhouettes_With_Deep_Generative_Networks.png/250px-Synthesizing_3D_Shapes_via_Modeling_Multi-View_Depth_Maps_and_Silhouettes_With_Deep_Generative_Networks.png)](/wiki/File:Synthesizing_3D_Shapes_via_Modeling_Multi-View_Depth_Maps_and_Silhouettes_With_Deep_Generative_Networks.png)Learning 3D shapes has been a challenging task in computer vision. Recent advances in [deep learning](/wiki/Deep_learning "Deep learning") have enabled researchers to build models that are able to generate and reconstruct 3D shapes from single or multi-view [depth maps](/wiki/Depth_map "Depth map") or silhouettes seamlessly and efficiently.[[25]](#cite_note-3DVAE-25)

  * Automatic inspection, _e.g._ , in manufacturing applications;
  * Assisting humans in identification tasks, e.g., a [species identification](/wiki/Automated_species_identification "Automated species identification") system;[[30]](#cite_note-30)
  * Controlling processes, _e.g._ , an [industrial robot](/wiki/Industrial_robots "Industrial robots");
  * [Detecting events](/wiki/Activity_recognition "Activity recognition"), _e.g._ , for [visual surveillance](/wiki/Artificial_intelligence_for_video_surveillance "Artificial intelligence for video surveillance") or [people counting](/wiki/People_counter "People counter"), e.g., in the [restaurant industry](/wiki/Presto_\(restaurant_technology_platform\) "Presto \(restaurant technology platform\)");
  * Interaction, _e.g._ , as the input to a device for [computer-human interaction](/wiki/Computer-human_interaction "Computer-human interaction");
  * monitoring agricultural crops, e.g. an open-source [vision transformers](/wiki/Vision_transformer "Vision transformer") model[[31]](#cite_note-31) has been developed to help farmers automatically detect [strawberry diseases](/wiki/List_of_strawberry_diseases "List of strawberry diseases") with 98.4% accuracy.[[32]](#cite_note-32)
  * Modeling objects or environments, _e.g._ , medical image analysis or [topographical](/wiki/Topographic_map "Topographic map") modeling;
  * Navigation, _e.g._ , by an [autonomous vehicle](/wiki/Autonomous_vehicle "Autonomous vehicle") or [mobile robot](/wiki/Mobile_robot "Mobile robot");
  * Organizing information, _e.g._ , for [indexing](/wiki/Search_engine_indexing "Search engine indexing") databases of images and image sequences.
  * Tracking surfaces or planes in 3D coordinates for allowing [Augmented Reality](/wiki/Augmented_reality "Augmented reality") experiences.
  * Analyzing the condition of facilities in industry or construction.
  * Automatic real-time lip-reading for devices and apps to assist people with disabilities.[[33]](#cite_note-33)

For 2024, the leading areas of computer vision were industry (market size US$5.22 billion),[[34]](#cite_note-34) medicine (market size US$2.6 billion),[[35]](#cite_note-35) military (market size US$996.2 million).[[36]](#cite_note-36)

### Medicine

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Medicine")]

[DARPA](/wiki/DARPA "DARPA")'s Visual Media Reasoning concept video

One of the most prominent application fields is [medical computer vision](/wiki/Medical_computer_vision "Medical computer vision"), or medical image processing, characterized by the extraction of information from image data to [diagnose a patient.](/wiki/Computer-assisted_diagnosis "Computer-assisted diagnosis")[[37]](#cite_note-37) An example of this is the detection of [tumours](/wiki/Tumour "Tumour"), [arteriosclerosis](/wiki/Arteriosclerosis "Arteriosclerosis") or other malign changes, and a variety of dental pathologies; measurements of organ dimensions, blood flow, etc. are another example. It also supports medical research by providing new information: _e.g._ , about the structure of the brain or the quality of medical treatments. Applications of computer vision in the medical area also include enhancement of images interpreted by humans—[ultrasonic images](/wiki/Ultrasound "Ultrasound") or [X-ray images](/wiki/Radiography "Radiography"), for example—to reduce the influence of noise. 

### Machine vision

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Machine vision")]

A second application area in computer vision is in industry, sometimes called [machine vision](/wiki/Machine_vision "Machine vision"), where information is extracted for the purpose of supporting a production process. One example is quality control where details or final products are being automatically inspected in order to find defects. One of the most prevalent fields for such inspection is the [Wafer](/wiki/Wafer_\(electronics\) "Wafer \(electronics\)") industry in which every single Wafer is being measured and inspected for inaccuracies or defects to prevent a [computer chip](/wiki/Integrated_circuit "Integrated circuit") from coming to market in an unusable manner. Another example is a measurement of the position and orientation of details to be picked up by a robot arm. Machine vision is also heavily used in the agricultural processes to remove undesirable foodstuff from bulk material, a process called [optical sorting](/wiki/Optical_sorting "Optical sorting").[[38]](#cite_note-Davies-2005-38)

### Military

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Military")]

The obvious examples are the detection of enemy soldiers or vehicles and [missile guidance](/wiki/Missile_guidance "Missile guidance"). More advanced systems for missile guidance send the missile to an area rather than a specific target, and target selection is made when the missile reaches the area based on locally acquired image data. Modern military concepts, such as "battlefield awareness", imply that various sensors, including image sensors, provide a rich set of information about a combat scene that can be used to support strategic decisions. In this case, automatic processing of the data is used to reduce complexity and to fuse information from multiple sensors to increase reliability.

### Tactile feedback

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Tactile feedback")]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Finger_sensor.webp/330px-Finger_sensor.webp.png)](/wiki/File:Finger_sensor.webp)Rubber artificial skin layer with the flexible structure for the shape estimation of micro-undulation surfaces [![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Silicon_Sensor.webp/330px-Silicon_Sensor.webp.png)](/wiki/File:Silicon_Sensor.webp)Above is a silicon mold with a camera inside containing many different point markers. When this sensor is pressed against the surface, the silicon deforms, and the position of the point markers shifts. A computer can then take this data and determine how exactly the mold is pressed against the surface. This can be used to calibrate robotic hands in order to make sure they can grasp objects effectively.

Materials such as rubber and silicon are being used to create sensors that allow for applications such as detecting microundulations and calibrating robotic hands. Rubber can be used in order to create a mold that can be placed over a finger, inside of this mold would be multiple strain gauges. The finger mold and sensors could then be placed on top of a small sheet of rubber containing an array of rubber pins. A user can then wear the finger mold and trace a surface. A computer can then read the data from the strain gauges and measure if one or more of the pins are being pushed upward. If a pin is being pushed upward then the computer can recognize this as an imperfection in the surface. This sort of technology is useful in order to receive accurate data on imperfections on a very large surface.[[39]](#cite_note-:0-39) Another variation of this finger mold sensor are sensors that contain a camera suspended in silicon. The silicon forms a dome around the outside of the camera and embedded in the silicon are point markers that are equally spaced. These cameras can then be placed on devices such as robotic hands in order to allow the computer to receive highly accurate tactile data.[[40]](#cite_note-:1-40)

  * Support of [visual effects](/wiki/Visual_effects "Visual effects") creation for cinema and broadcast, _e.g._ , [camera tracking](/wiki/Camera_tracking "Camera tracking") (match moving).
  * [Surveillance](/wiki/Surveillance "Surveillance").
  * [Driver drowsiness detection](/wiki/Driver_drowsiness_detection "Driver drowsiness detection")[[41]](#cite_note-41)[[42]](#cite_note-42)[[43]](#cite_note-43)
  * Tracking and counting organisms in the biological sciences[[44]](#cite_note-BruijningVisser2018-44)

## Typical tasks

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Typical tasks")]

Each of the application areas described above employ a range of computer vision tasks; more or less well-defined measurement problems or processing problems, which can be solved using a variety of methods. Some examples of typical computer vision tasks are presented below. 

Computer vision tasks include methods for [acquiring](/wiki/Image_sensor "Image sensor"), [processing](/wiki/Image_processing "Image processing"), [analyzing](/wiki/Image_analysis "Image analysis") and understanding digital images, and extraction of [high-dimensional](/wiki/High-dimensional "High-dimensional") data from the real world in order to produce numerical or symbolic information, _e.g._ , in the forms of decisions.[[1]](#cite_note-Klette-2014-1)[[2]](#cite_note-Shapiro-Stockman-2001-2)[[3]](#cite_note-Morris-2004-3)[[4]](#cite_note-Jahne-Haussecker-2000-4) Understanding in this context means the transformation of visual images (the input of the retina) into descriptions of the world that can interface with other thought processes and elicit appropriate action. This image understanding can be seen as the disentangling of symbolic information from image data using models constructed with the aid of geometry, physics, statistics, and learning theory.[[45]](#cite_note-Forsyth-Ponce-2003-45)

### Recognition

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Recognition")]

The classical problem in computer vision, image processing, and [machine vision](/wiki/Machine_vision "Machine vision") is that of determining whether or not the image data contains some specific object, feature, or activity. Different varieties of recognition problem are described in the literature.[[46]](#cite_note-Forsyth2012-46)

  * **[Object recognition](/wiki/Object_recognition "Object recognition")** (also called **object classification**) – one or several pre-specified or learned objects or object classes can be recognized, usually together with their 2D positions in the image or 3D poses in the scene. Blippar, [Google Goggles](/wiki/Google_Goggles "Google Goggles"), and LikeThat provide stand-alone programs that illustrate this functionality.
  * **Identification** – an individual instance of an object is recognized. Examples include identification of a specific person's face or fingerprint, [identification of handwritten digits](/wiki/Handwriting_recognition "Handwriting recognition"), or the identification of a specific vehicle.
  * **[Detection](/wiki/Object_detection "Object detection")** – the image data are scanned for specific objects along with their locations. Examples include the detection of an obstacle in the car's field of view and possible abnormal cells or tissues in medical images or the detection of a vehicle in an automatic road toll system. Detection based on relatively simple and fast computations is sometimes used for finding smaller regions of interesting image data which can be further analyzed by more computationally demanding techniques to produce a correct interpretation.

Currently, the best algorithms for such tasks are based on [convolutional neural networks](/wiki/Convolutional_neural_network "Convolutional neural network"). An illustration of their capabilities is given by the [ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge](/wiki/ImageNet#ImageNet_Challenge "ImageNet"); this is a benchmark in object classification and detection, with millions of images and 1000 object classes used in the competition.[[47]](#cite_note-:2-47) Performance of convolutional neural networks on the ImageNet tests is now close to that of humans.[[47]](#cite_note-:2-47) The best algorithms still struggle with objects that are small or thin, such as a small ant on the stem of a flower or a person holding a quill in their hand. They also have trouble with images that have been distorted with filters (an increasingly common phenomenon with modern digital cameras). By contrast, those kinds of images rarely trouble humans. Humans, however, tend to have trouble with other issues. For example, they are not good at classifying objects into fine-grained classes, such as the particular breed of dog or species of bird, whereas convolutional neural networks handle this with ease.[_[citation needed](/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Several specialized tasks based on recognition exist, such as: 

  * **[Content-based image retrieval](/wiki/Content-based_image_retrieval "Content-based image retrieval")** – finding all images in a larger set of images which have a specific content. The content can be specified in different ways, for example in 
[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/SRT_Shape_Recognition_Technology.png/250px-SRT_Shape_Recognition_Technology.png)](/wiki/File:SRT_Shape_Recognition_Technology.png)Computer vision for [people counter](/wiki/People_counter "People counter") purposes in public places, malls, shopping centers

  * **[Pose estimation](/wiki/Pose_\(computer_vision\) "Pose \(computer vision\)")** – estimating the position or orientation of a specific object relative to the camera. An example application for this technique would be assisting a robot arm in retrieving objects from a conveyor belt in an [assembly line](/wiki/Assembly_line "Assembly line") situation or picking parts from a bin.
  * **[Optical character recognition](/wiki/Optical_character_recognition "Optical character recognition")** (OCR) – identifying [characters](/wiki/Character_\(computing\) "Character \(computing\)") in images of printed or handwritten text, usually with a view to encoding the text in a format more amenable to editing or [indexing](/wiki/Search_index "Search index") (_e.g._ [ASCII](/wiki/ASCII "ASCII")). A related task is reading of 2D codes such as [data matrix](/wiki/Data_Matrix "Data Matrix") and [QR](/wiki/QR_code "QR code") codes.
  * **[Facial recognition](/wiki/Facial_recognition_system "Facial recognition system") – ** a technology that enables the matching of faces in digital images or video frames to a face database, which is now widely used for mobile phone facelock, smart door locking, etc.[[48]](#cite_note-48)
  * [Emotion recognition](/wiki/Emotion_recognition "Emotion recognition")** – **a subset of facial recognition, emotion recognition refers to the process of classifying human [emotions.](/wiki/Emotion "Emotion") Psychologists caution, however, that internal emotions cannot be reliably detected from faces.[[49]](#cite_note-49)
  * **[Shape Recognition Technology](/wiki/Pattern_recognition "Pattern recognition")** (SRT) in [people counter](/wiki/People_counter "People counter") systems differentiating human beings (head and shoulder patterns) from objects.
  * **[Human activity recognition](/wiki/Activity_recognition "Activity recognition")** \- deals with recognizing the activity from a series of video frames, such as, if the person is picking up an object or walking.

### Motion analysis

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Motion analysis")]

Several tasks relate to motion estimation, where an image sequence is processed to produce an estimate of the velocity either at each points in the image or in the 3D scene or even of the camera that produces the images. Examples of such tasks are: 

  * **[Egomotion](/wiki/Egomotion "Egomotion")** – determining the 3D rigid motion (rotation and translation) of the camera from an image sequence produced by the camera.
  * **[Tracking](/wiki/Video_tracking "Video tracking")** – following the movements of a (usually) smaller set of interest points or objects (_e.g._ , vehicles, objects, humans or other organisms[[44]](#cite_note-BruijningVisser2018-44)) in the image sequence. This has vast industry applications as most high-running machinery can be monitored in this way.
  * **[Optical flow](/wiki/Optical_flow "Optical flow")** – to determine, for each point in the image, how that point is moving relative to the image plane, _i.e._ , its apparent motion. This motion is a result of both how the corresponding 3D point is moving in the scene and how the camera is moving relative to the scene.

### Scene reconstruction

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Scene reconstruction")]

Given one or (typically) more images of a scene, or a video, scene reconstruction aims at [computing a 3D model](/wiki/3D_reconstruction "3D reconstruction") of the scene. In the simplest case, the model can be a set of 3D points. More sophisticated methods produce a complete 3D surface model. The advent of 3D imaging not requiring motion or scanning, and related processing algorithms is enabling rapid advances in this field. Grid-based 3D sensing can be used to acquire 3D images from multiple angles. Algorithms are now available to stitch multiple 3D images together into point clouds and 3D models.[[25]](#cite_note-3DVAE-25)

### Image restoration

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Image restoration")]

Image restoration comes into the picture when the original image is degraded or damaged due to some external factors like lens wrong positioning, transmission interference, low lighting or motion blurs, etc., which is referred to as noise. When the images are degraded or damaged, the information to be extracted from them also gets damaged. Therefore, we need to recover or restore the image as it was intended to be. The aim of image restoration is the removal of noise (sensor noise, motion blur, etc.) from images. The simplest possible approach for noise removal is various types of filters, such as low-pass filters or median filters. More sophisticated methods assume a model of how the local image structures look to distinguish them from noise. By first analyzing the image data in terms of the local image structures, such as lines or edges, and then controlling the filtering based on local information from the analysis step, a better level of noise removal is usually obtained compared to the simpler approaches. 

An example in this field is [inpainting](/wiki/Inpainting "Inpainting"). 

## System methods

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: System methods")]

The organization of a computer vision system is highly application-dependent. Some systems are stand-alone applications that solve a specific measurement or detection problem, while others constitute a sub-system of a larger design which, for example, also contains sub-systems for control of mechanical actuators, planning, information databases, man-machine interfaces, etc. The specific implementation of a computer vision system also depends on whether its functionality is pre-specified or if some part of it can be learned or modified during operation. Many functions are unique to the application. There are, however, typical functions that are found in many computer vision systems. 

  * **Image acquisition** – A digital image is produced by one or several [image sensors](/wiki/Image_sensor "Image sensor"), which, besides various types of light-sensitive cameras, include [range sensors](/wiki/Rangefinder_camera "Rangefinder camera"), tomography devices, radar, ultra-sonic cameras, etc. Depending on the type of sensor, the resulting image data is an ordinary 2D image, a 3D volume, or an image sequence. The pixel values typically correspond to light intensity in one or several spectral bands (gray images or colour images) but can also be related to various physical measures, such as depth, absorption or reflectance of sonic or electromagnetic waves, or [magnetic resonance imaging](/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging "Magnetic resonance imaging").[[38]](#cite_note-Davies-2005-38)
  * **Pre-processing** – Before a computer vision method can be applied to image data in order to extract some specific piece of information, it is usually necessary to process the data in order to ensure that it satisfies certain assumptions implied by the method. Examples are: 
    * Re-sampling to ensure that the image coordinate system is correct.
    * Noise reduction to ensure that sensor noise does not introduce false information.
    * Contrast enhancement to ensure that relevant information can be detected.
    * [Scale space](/wiki/Scale_space "Scale space") representation to enhance image structures at locally appropriate scales.
  * **[Feature extraction](/wiki/Feature_detection_\(computer_vision\) "Feature detection \(computer vision\)")** – Image features at various levels of complexity are extracted from the image data.[[38]](#cite_note-Davies-2005-38) Typical examples of such features are: 
    * Lines, [edges](/wiki/Edge_detection "Edge detection") and [ridges](/wiki/Ridge_detection "Ridge detection").
    * Localized [interest points](/wiki/Interest_point_detection "Interest point detection") such as [corners](/wiki/Corner_detection "Corner detection"), [blobs](/wiki/Blob_detection "Blob detection") or points.

    More complex features may be related to texture, shape, or motion.

  * **[Detection](/wiki/Object_detection "Object detection")/[segmentation](/wiki/Image_segmentation "Image segmentation")** – At some point in the processing, a decision is made about which image points or regions of the image are relevant for further processing.[[38]](#cite_note-Davies-2005-38) Examples are: 
    * Selection of a specific set of interest points.
    * Segmentation of one or multiple image regions that contain a specific object of interest.
    * Segmentation of image into nested scene architecture comprising foreground, object groups, single objects or [salient](/wiki/Salience_\(neuroscience\) "Salience \(neuroscience\)") object[[50]](#cite_note-50) parts (also referred to as spatial-taxon scene hierarchy),[[51]](#cite_note-51) while the [visual salience](/wiki/Salience_\(neuroscience\) "Salience \(neuroscience\)") is often implemented as [spatial](/wiki/Visual_spatial_attention "Visual spatial attention") and [temporal attention](/wiki/Visual_temporal_attention "Visual temporal attention").
    * Segmentation or [co-segmentation](/wiki/Object_co-segmentation "Object co-segmentation") of one or multiple videos into a series of per-frame foreground masks while maintaining its temporal semantic continuity.[[52]](#cite_note-Liu_Wang_Hua_Zhang_2018_pp._5840–5853-52)[[53]](#cite_note-Wang_Duan_Zhang_Niu_p=1657-53)
  * **High-level processing** – At this step, the input is typically a small set of data, for example, a set of points or an image region, which is assumed to contain a specific object.[[38]](#cite_note-Davies-2005-38) The remaining processing deals with, for example: 
    * Verification that the data satisfies model-based and application-specific assumptions.
    * Estimation of application-specific parameters, such as object pose or object size.
    * [Image recognition](/wiki/Image_recognition "Image recognition") – classifying a detected object into different categories.
    * [Image registration](/wiki/Image_registration "Image registration") – comparing and combining two different views of the same object.
  * **Decision making** Making the final decision required for the application,[[38]](#cite_note-Davies-2005-38) for example: 
    * Pass/fail on automatic inspection applications.
    * Match/no-match in recognition applications.
    * Flag for further human review in medical, military, security and recognition applications.

### Image-understanding systems

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Image-understanding systems")]

Image-understanding systems (IUS) include three levels of abstraction as follows: low level includes image primitives such as edges, texture elements, or regions; intermediate level includes boundaries, surfaces and volumes; and high level includes objects, scenes, or events. Many of these requirements are entirely topics for further research. 

The representational requirements in the designing of IUS for these levels are: representation of prototypical concepts, concept organization, spatial knowledge, temporal knowledge, scaling, and description by comparison and differentiation. 

While inference refers to the process of deriving new, not explicitly represented facts from currently known facts, control refers to the process that selects which of the many inference, search, and matching techniques should be applied at a particular stage of processing. Inference and control requirements for IUS are: search and hypothesis activation, matching and hypothesis testing, generation and use of expectations, change and focus of attention, certainty and strength of belief, inference and goal satisfaction.[[54]](#cite_note-54)

## Hardware

[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Computer_vision&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Hardware")]

[![](//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/LiDAR_Scanner_and_Back_Camera_of_iPad_Pro_2020_-_3.jpg/250px-LiDAR_Scanner_and_Back_Camera_of_iPad_Pro_2020_-_3.jpg)](/wiki/File:LiDAR_Scanner_and_Back_Camera_of_iPad_Pro_2020_-_3.jpg)A [2020 model iPad Pro](/wiki/IPad_Pro_\(4th_generation\) "IPad Pro \(4th generation\)") with a [LiDAR](/wiki/LiDAR "LiDAR") sensor

There are many kinds of computer vision systems; however, all of them contain these basic elements: a power source, at least one image acquisition device (camera, ccd, etc.), a processor, and control and communication cables or some kind of wireless interconnection mechanism. In addition, a practical vision system contains software, as well as a display in order to monitor the system. Vision systems for inner spaces, as most industrial ones, contain an illumination system and may be placed in a controlled environment. Furthermore, a completed system includes many accessories, such as camera supports, cables, and connectors. 

Most computer vision systems use visible-light cameras passively viewing a scene at frame rates of at most 60 frames per second (usually far slower). 

A few computer vision systems use image-acquisition hardware with active illumination or something other than visible light or both, such as [structured-light 3D scanners](/wiki/Structured-light_3D_scanner "Structured-light 3D scanner"), [thermographic cameras](/wiki/Thermographic_camera "Thermographic camera"), [hyperspectral imagers](/wiki/Hyperspectral_imager "Hyperspectral imager"), [radar imaging](/wiki/Radar_imaging "Radar imaging"), [lidar](/wiki/Lidar "Lidar") scanners, [magnetic resonance images](/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_image "Magnetic resonance image"), [side-scan sonar](/wiki/Side-scan_sonar "Side-scan sonar"), [synthetic aperture sonar](/wiki/Synthetic_aperture_sonar "Synthetic aperture sonar"), etc. Such hardware captures "images" that are then processed often using the same computer vision algorithms used to process visible-light images. 

While traditional broadcast and consumer video systems operate at a rate of 30 frames per second, advances in [digital signal processing](/wiki/Digital_signal_processing "Digital signal processing") and [consumer graphics hardware](/wiki/Graphics_processing_unit "Graphics processing unit") has made high-speed image acquisition, processing, and display possible for real-time systems on the order of hundreds to thousands of frames per second. For applications in robotics, fast, real-time video systems are critically important and often can simplify the processing needed for certain algorithms. When combined with a high-speed projector, fast image acquisition allows 3D measurement and feature tracking to be realized.[[55]](#cite_note-55)

[Egocentric vision](/wiki/Egocentric_vision "Egocentric vision") systems are composed of a wearable camera that automatically take pictures from a first-person perspective. 

As of 2016, [vision processing units](/wiki/Vision_processing_unit "Vision processing unit") are emerging as a new class of processors to complement CPUs and [graphics processing units](/wiki/Graphics_processing_units "Graphics processing units") (GPUs) in this role.[[56]](#cite_note-the_rise_of_VPUs-56)
